# User Research Guide — 45-Minute Interview Structure + JTBD Extraction + Synthesis

**A complete guide to conducting 3–5 user interviews per persona to validate problem assumptions**

---

## Pre-Interview: Recruitment & Screeners

### Recruitment Criteria

**Recruit for BEHAVIOR, not demographics.**

| Scenario | Recruit For |
|---|---|
| Validating a problem exists | People who currently do the job (even if using workarounds) |
| Testing a solution | People who have tried a similar solution and either use it or rejected it |
| Segmenting users | Different user personas doing the same job differently |

**Example screener questions:**
1. "In your role, do you spend time tracking [the job]?" (Filter for people who actually do the work)
2. "How much time per week?" (Ensure frequency is high enough to be painful)
3. "What solution do you use today?" (Understand their current workaround)
4. "Have you tried [competitor name]?" (Segment between power users and non-adopters)

### Recruitment Channels

- LinkedIn (direct message people with target job title)
- Email lists (purchase or assemble from LinkedIn)
- Community forums (r/[industry], Slack groups, industry associations)
- Referrals (ask existing customers to refer similar companies)
- Cold email (10% response rate is normal for B2B cold email to relevant personas)

### Target Sample Size

- **3–5 interviews per persona** for problem validation
- **At least 1 persona per user segment** (don't just interview happy users)

### Incentive & Setup

- Offer $25–$50 Amazon gift card for 1-hour interview
- Schedule 60 minutes (50 min interview + 10 min buffer)
- Use Zoom or in-person (in-person is higher quality)
- Record with permission (transcribe for notes)

## The 45-Minute Interview Script

**Total time:** 50 minutes (5-min buffer included)

### Section 1: Warm-Up (5 minutes)

**Goal:** Build rapport; establish context

**Script:**
> "Thanks for taking time to talk. I'm [name] at [company]. We're building a tool to help people like you with [problem area]. I'd love to understand your day-to-day better — specifically around [the job]. Sound good?"

**Key questions:**
1. "Tell me about your role and how long you've been doing it."
2. "What's your primary responsibility around [the job]?"

**Listen for:** Role title, tenure (longer tenure = deeper expertise), company context

**Do not:** Pitch your solution yet. Do not mention your product.

---

### Section 2: Workflow (15 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand exactly what they do today (before suggesting a solution)

**Script:**
> "I'd like to understand your day-to-day. Can you walk me through the last time you [did the job]? Start from the beginning and tell me everything you did."

**Key questions:**
1. "Walk me through exactly what you did. Don't skip steps."
2. "What tools did you use?" (Observe: spreadsheet? Email? Paper? Specialized software?)
3. "How long did it take?"
4. "Did anything go wrong or frustrate you?"
5. "Is this how you do it every time, or do you vary the approach?"

**Listen for:** 
- Manual steps (alert for pain points)
- Workarounds (they're telling you their real competition)
- Variance in process (where's the confusion?)
- Emotional signals ("I hate this part," "this is tedious")

**Do not:** Suggest better ways to do it. Do not interrupt. Let them describe the full flow.

---

### Section 3: Pain (15 minutes)

**Goal:** Quantify the cost of the problem (time, money, error rate)

**Script:**
> "You mentioned [the frustrating step]. Tell me more about that. When has this gone wrong?"

**Key questions:**
1. "When have you gotten this wrong or missed something?" (Look for specific incident, not general complaint)
2. "How often does that happen?" (Frequency)
3. "What happened as a result?" (Cost: did it cost money? time? trust?)
4. "How much did that cost [in time / money / consequences]?" (Quantify)
5. "How many times has this happened to you?" (Frequency over time)
6. "Is there a worse case than the one you described?" (Listen for maximum impact)

**Listen for:**
- Specific incidents (not hypothetical)
- Quantified costs ($10K mistake, 5 hours/week wasted, 3 times per year)
- Emotional impact ("I was stressed," "looked bad to my team")
- Frequency (once a year vs. once a week = different priority)

**Do not:** Dismiss their pain. Do not offer solutions yet.

**Example:**
- Bad: "So that's frustrating?" (Leading)
- Good: "Tell me about the last time this happened." (Open)

---

### Section 4: Current Workarounds (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand what they hire today to do this job (your real competition)

**Script:**
> "So you mentioned you use [tool]. Tell me how that works for you today. What's good about it? What's not?"

**Key questions:**
1. "What are you using today to manage this?" (Don't assume you know)
2. "How well does [tool] work for this specific job?" (1–10 scale)
3. "What do you like about it?" (Don't let them only complain)
4. "What frustrates you most?" (Be specific)
5. "Have you looked at alternatives?" (Signal of switching motivation)
6. "What would make you switch?" (Direct switching intent question)

**Listen for:**
- Multiple tools stitched together ("I use Excel + Outlook + property manager calls") = underserved job
- Switching intent ("If there was a better option, I'd switch immediately") = market opportunity
- Satisfaction ("I'm pretty happy with it, but...") = low switching motivation

**Do not:** Mention your product. Do not ask "Would you use X?"

---

### Section 5: Solution Validation (5 minutes)

**Goal:** Gauge interest in a solution; NOT to close them on your product

**Script:**
> "If I told you someone built a tool that could [solve the specific pain you mentioned], would that interest you?"

**Key questions:**
1. "How important is this problem to you?" (1–10 scale)
2. "Would you pay for a tool that solved this?" (Direct WTP question)
3. "How much would you pay per month?" (Price range)
4. "[If they say yes] What would need to be true for you to actually switch?" (Signal of switching cost)

**Listen for:**
- Enthusiasm (not just politeness) about a solution
- Willingness to pay (WTP) and price sensitivity
- Feature requirements ("Only if it also tracks...") 
- Switching barriers ("Our company is locked into CoStar for 2 more years")

**Do not:** Pitch your specific product. Do not oversell. Do not make promises.

## Post-Interview: JTBD Extraction

**Immediately after the call, extract 3 jobs for this persona:**

### Functional Job (The Task)

**Definition:** What task are they trying to accomplish? What's the objective?

**Examples:**
- "Track lease renewals so we don't miss 90-day notice windows"
- "Ensure compliance with multi-state lease regulations"
- "Analyze lease portfolio to negotiate better renewal rates"

**How to extract:** Listen to what they said they "do" in Section 2. That's the functional job.

### Emotional Job (The Feeling)

**Definition:** How do they want to feel while doing this task or after accomplishing it?

**Examples:**
- "Feel in control and not blindsided by lease deadlines"
- "Look organized and proactive to my team"
- "Feel confident that we're compliant and won't face audit findings"

**How to extract:** Listen to the emotional language ("I was stressed," "felt bad," "worried"). Invert negative emotions to positive desired states.

### Social Job (The Status)

**Definition:** How do they want to be perceived by others?

**Examples:**
- "Be seen as organized and detail-oriented, not someone who misses deadlines"
- "Present data-driven analysis to leadership (not scrambling at last minute)"
- "Be the expert on compliance so my team trusts me"

**How to extract:** Listen for references to how their work affects their standing ("my team thinks I'm...", "my boss expects...", "I need to look like...").

### Template

After each interview, fill this out:

```
Persona: [Name/Role]

Functional Job:
[What they're trying to accomplish]

Emotional Job:
[How they want to feel]

Social Job:
[How they want to be perceived]

Key Pain Point:
[Most acute problem + quantified cost]

Workaround:
[What they use today]

WTP Signal:
[Price they'd pay + switching motivation]
```

## Interview Synthesis (After 3–5 interviews)

### Cross-Interview Theme Extraction

**Method:** Read through all interview notes and tag each observation.

**Tags (examples):**
- `manual_work` — observation about time spent on manual tasks
- `spreadsheet_pain` — complaints about using spreadsheets
- `compliance_gap` — observation about regulatory gaps
- `switching_cost` — mention of switching barriers

**Goal:** Find themes that appear in 3+ interviews = validated signal

**Example synthesis:**

| Theme | Frequency | Evidence | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Manual tracking is time-intensive** | 5/5 interviews | "3–5 hours/week on spreadsheet tracking" | Table-stakes requirement: automation is expected |
| **Missed deadlines are costly** | 4/5 interviews | "Cost us $40K," "worried about missing renewals," "happened 2x last year" | WTP is driven by cost avoidance |
| **Jurisdiction compliance is confusing** | 3/5 interviews | "Different rules per state," "I maintain 3 spreadsheets," "not sure if compliant" | Differentiation opportunity: jurisdiction expertise |
| **Current tools are inadequate** | 5/5 interviews | "CoStar is overkill," "spreadsheet is unreliable," "no better options" | Market opportunity: new entrant can win |

### Validated Assumptions

After synthesis, create a table of assumptions and whether they're validated:

| Assumption | Hypothesis | Evidence | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Problem is acute** | Users spend 3+ hours/week + miss deadlines | 5/5 users mention both | ✅ Validated |
| **WTP is $250–$500/month** | Users can justify $3K–$6K/year | 4/5 willing at $300+; 1/5 at $150 | ✅ Mostly Validated |
| **Current solutions are inadequate** | CoStar/spreadsheet don't fully solve | 5/5 mention frustrations | ✅ Validated |
| **Jurisdiction compliance is table-stakes** | Users need multi-state support | 3/5 mention multi-state; 2/5 single-state | ⚠️ Partial (not 100%) |

### Recommendation: Proceed or Pivot?

**Proceed to PRD if:**
- 80%+ of assumptions are validated
- No Tier 1 assumptions failed
- You have clear user stories + requirements

**Pivot if:**
- Key assumptions failed (WTP is $100/month, not $300)
- Problem is less acute than expected (1 hour/week, not 3–5)
- Different persona is more motivated (legal officers care more than admins)

**Kill if:**
- Multiple Tier 1 assumptions failed
- No path forward is clear

## Interview Quality Checklist

After each interview, rate yourself:

- [ ] Did you let them talk 70%+ of the time? (You should talk 30%, they 70%)
- [ ] Did you avoid pitching your product? (Good interviews feel like conversations, not sales calls)
- [ ] Did you ask "why" 3+ times to get to root causes?
- [ ] Did you hear about a specific incident? (Not just general complaints)
- [ ] Did you get quantified impact? (Cost, time, or frequency)
- [ ] Did you understand their workaround?
- [ ] Did you test their WTP directly?

**If you said "no" to >2 items, the interview quality is low. Schedule a re-do or discount the findings.**

## Common Mistakes

1. **Asking leading questions:** 
   - Bad: "Don't you hate having to manually track leases?"
   - Good: "Tell me about the last time you tracked leases."

2. **Pitching too early:**
   - Bad: "We're building a tool that automatically tracks renewals. Would you use it?"
   - Good: "Tell me how you track renewals today."

3. **Interviewing only happy users:**
   - Missing signal from churned users, non-adopters, and power users of competitors
   - **Fix:** Recruit 1–2 users who tried an alternative and left, or who use workarounds

4. **Not quantifying pain:**
   - Bad: "That's frustrating."
   - Good: "How much time does that waste? How often does it happen?"

5. **Skipping the "what would make you switch" question:**
   - Missing switching intent and barriers
   - **Fix:** Always ask directly
